# Writing Explanation Documentation

Explanation is a **discursive treatment** of a subject that permits **reflection**. Explanation is **understanding-oriented**.

It deepens and broadens the reader's understanding. It brings **clarity, light, and context**. It answers the question: *"Can you tell me about...?"*

Explanation is the only kind of documentation that might make sense to read **away from the product itself** — in the bath, on the train, over coffee.

## The Value of Explanation

### Explanation and Understanding

Explanation is characterized by its **distance from active concerns**. It doesn't have direct implications for what the practitioner does. This makes it sometimes seem less important — but that's a mistake.

**It may be less urgent than the other three, but it is no less important.**

No practitioner of a craft can afford to be without understanding of that craft. Understanding seals together the other components of mastery and makes it safely our own. Without it, knowledge is **loose, fragmented, fragile**, and practice is **anxious**.

### Explanation by Any Other Name

Your explanation docs don't need to be called "Explanation." Alternatives include:

- Discussion
- Background
- Conceptual guides
- Topics
- Deep dives
- Architecture guides

The word "explanation" refers to **unfolding** — the revelation of what is hidden in the folds.

## Explanation's Boundaries

Explanation is often the hardest to scope. Tutorials are scoped by what you need the learner to learn. How-to guides are scoped by the task. Reference is scoped by the machinery itself.

For explanation, it's useful to have a real or imagined **"why" question** as a prompt. Otherwise, draw reasonable lines and be satisfied.

## Key Principles

### Make Connections

When writing explanation, you are helping to weave **a web of understanding**. Make connections to other things — even things outside the immediate topic — if that helps.

### Provide Context

Provide **background and context**:

- Why things are so
- Design decisions
- Historical reasons
- Technical constraints
- Implications
- Specific examples

### Talk About the Subject

Explanation guides are **about** a topic in the sense that they are **around** it.

Things to discuss:

- The bigger picture
- History
- Choices, alternatives, possibilities
- Why: reasons and justifications

Your titles should work with an implicit "About..." prefix:

- "About user authentication"
- "About database connection policies"
- "About the event-driven architecture"

### Admit Opinion and Perspective

All human activity is invested with opinion, beliefs, and thoughts. The reality of any human creation is rich with opinion, and that needs to be part of understanding.

Any understanding comes from a **perspective** — which means other perspectives exist. Explanation can and must consider alternatives, counter-examples, and multiple approaches.

In explanation, you're **not** giving instruction or describing facts. You're **opening up the topic for consideration**. Think of it as **discussion** — discussions can weigh up contrary opinions.

### Keep Explanation Closely Bounded

One risk of explanation is that it **absorbs other things**. The writer feels the urge to include instruction or technical description. But documentation already has other places for these.

**Don't let how-to or reference creep in.** Allowing them interferes with the explanation and removes them from their correct place.

## Language Patterns

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Explain reasons | "The reason for x is because historically, y..." |
| Offer judgments | "W is better than z, because..." |
| Provide context | "An x in system y is analogous to a w in system z. However..." |
| Weigh alternatives | "Some users prefer w (because z). This can be a good approach, but..." |
| Unfold internals | "An x interacts with a y as follows:..." |

## Common Explanation Topics for Software

- Architecture and design decisions
- Why a particular technology or approach was chosen
- How the system evolved and why
- Security model and threat modeling rationale
- Trade-offs made and alternatives considered
- Conceptual models and mental frameworks
- How subsystems relate to each other
- The problem space the software addresses

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Including step-by-step instructions (that's a how-to guide)
- Providing reference-level technical details (that's reference)
- Leaving explanation scattered across tutorials and how-to guides
- Failing to draw boundaries — explanation that tries to cover everything
- Presenting only one perspective without acknowledging alternatives
- Writing explanation that has no clear "about" topic
- Pretending to be neutral when opinions are actually present and useful
